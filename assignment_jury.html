About Ikat

Where threads become living patterns

Ikat is not just woven; it is planned, tied, dyed, aligned, and patiently brought to life. Before the fabric appears, the design already lives inside the yarn.

Every blurred edge, bold motif, and colour shift carries the touch of an artisan who understands rhythm, patience, and precision.

In Lingala Ghanpur, this craft grows from a close community of skilled weavers. The Padmashali weaving tradition, women's contribution in preparation and dyeing, and the careful work of loom artisans together keep this heritage alive.

People Behind the Craft

Artisans of Lingala Ghanpur

The final textile carries the work of many hands. These profiles from the field documentation show the weavers, assistants, and elders who keep the Ikat tradition moving across generations.

Maheswaram Revathi

FemaleAge 27Weaver

Home-based Ikat weaving

Revathi is one of the few women in Lingala Ghanpur engaged in weaving, a physically demanding task traditionally done by men. A graduate and mother of two, she works with her husband across yarn preparation, dyeing, and weaving.

Maheswaram Raghunandhan

MaleAge 39Weaver

Third-generation master weaver

Raghunandhan formerly managed a team of nine artisans in his family workshop. He now contributes to a government initiative developing lighter, softer fabrics while preserving the traditional character of Ikat.

Maheswaram Srinivas

MaleAge 40Weaver

Mobile weaving support

Srinivas is a full-time Ikat weaver whose work involves travelling between locations to complete different stages of the weaving process. He is valued for steady support within the village weaving network.

Bitala Uppendra

FemaleAge 35Weaver's Assistant

Pre-loom preparation

Uppendra learned Ikat work by assisting family members from childhood. She supports yarn preparation and dyeing, passes the skills to her children, and helps maintain the quality of her family's home-based practice.

Maheswaram Somalakshmi

FemaleAge 80Retired Weaver

Knowledge keeper

With over 40 years of experience, Somalakshmi worked across Ikat stages, weaving silk sarees, cotton fabrics, dhotis, and towels. Her knowledge has passed to children and grandchildren who continue the craft.

Maheswaram Somaya

MaleAge 92Retired Weaver

Elder of the weaving community

Somaya moved from rice mill labour and agriculture into weaving dhotis at home. His journey helped a small practice grow into a weaving community that continues through later generations.

Making Process

How Ikat Is Made

Every step matters because the pattern is prepared in the yarn before the fabric is woven.

1

Yarn Boiling

Threads are cleaned and softened so they can absorb colour evenly during dyeing.

2

Winding

Yarn is wound carefully onto bobbins, making it easier to handle, measure, and prepare.

3

Design Marking

The pattern is marked directly on the stretched yarn before the cloth is woven.

4

Tying

Selected portions are tied tightly so those parts resist dye and preserve the motif.

5

Dyeing

Yarns are dipped in colour baths, sometimes in several layers, to build the final palette.

6

Drying

The dyed yarns are dried, checked, and arranged before they move to the loom.

7

Weaving

The weaver aligns every dyed thread so the planned design appears in the final fabric.
